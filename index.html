The Welfare Resilience Framework

Four dimensions of institutional capacity

The framework links three elements in sequence: economic risks created by technological change, policy instruments intended to address those risks, and the enabling infrastructure required to deliver those policies effectively. Its central premise is that policy ambition must be matched by institutional capacity.

Disruptive Risk
Short-term shocks such as sudden income loss, unstable hours, or rapid firm-level restructuring. These test a system's coping capacity, meaning its ability to stabilise households quickly and prevent hardship when labour market conditions shift abruptly.
Transitional Risks
Risks that arise over the medium term as tasks, occupations and sectors reconfigure. These require adaptive capacity: institutions capable of supporting worker mobility, re-skilling and reallocation as the structure of employment evolves.
Structural Risks
Risks that emerge gradually through long-run shifts in the relationship between labour, wages and productivity. These test transformative capacity, whether fiscal and welfare institutions are deep enough to manage persistent inequality, regional divergence or changes in how economic value is distributed.
Access & Delivery Risks
Systemic capacity which includes the administrative, legal, fiscal and digital systems that allow policies to function in practice. The framework emphasises that even well-designed policies will fail if these enabling conditions are weak.

01CopingDisruptive Risks
Core Risk — The Missing Middle
Brazil protects the poorest households and formal workers in the short run — but not the gap between them
Three disruptive risks dominate. First, about 40% of Brazil's workforce is informal and ineligible for contributory protections; 1.7 million platform workers are growing rapidly, with 64.1% contributing nothing to social security (IBGE, 2025). Second, banking is the clearest near-term formal-sector case: 7,473 banking jobs eliminated in the 12 months to Q1 2025, with core banking activities down 9,105 while IT rose only 1,842 (Contraf-CUT, 2025). Third, 35.4% of female workers are in AI-exposed occupations versus 25.2% of male workers (Barbosa Filho et al., 2025). No single programme automatically covers displaced formal workers above the Bolsa Família threshold — the "missing middle" numbers in the millions, with 8.4 million formal workers already registered in CadÚnico but no longer receiving Bolsa Família as a proxy for its scale.
What already exists
Bolsa Família + CadÚnico▾
Federal cash transfer reaching 20.8 million families (~55 million people) via the national social registry of 96.8 million. The Regra de Proteção lets enrolled families keep 50% of the benefit for up to 12 months after income rises above the threshold — tightened mid-2025. A strong floor, but not a rules-based response to routine displacement for workers above the threshold who were never enrolled.
Seguro Desemprego▾
3–5 months of unemployment insurance for formal workers dismissed without cause, at roughly 80% of prior wages, delivered via Caixa. Designed for cyclical unemployment, not structural displacement: the window typically closes before AI-driven re-employment is secured, and informal workers are excluded entirely.
FGTS▾
Mandatory employer-funded severance reserve: 8% of monthly salary deposited into an individual Caixa account, accessible on dismissal without cause. Supplements Seguro Desemprego as a forced savings buffer, but was designed for cyclical unemployment and does not address prolonged structural displacement.
Auxílio Emergencial (2020)▾
Emergency COVID transfer reaching ~66 million beneficiaries within weeks via CadÚnico and Caixa. Proof of concept that rapid, large-scale cash mobilisation is technically feasible — but required extraordinary political authorisation. No standing rule replicates this capacity under normal conditions.
Policy priorities
  • Actively communicate the Regra de Proteção as a limited re-employment bridge for low-income formal workers cycling in and out of Bolsa Família — no new legislation required.
  • Extend the banking-sector CCT retraining model to other high-exposure sectors through union-level negotiations. Not directly portable to retail, BPO, and back-office services where union density is much weaker.
  • Operationalize Seguro Desemprego delivery via Caixa branches for populations excluded from digital channels.
  • A standing income bridge for the missing middle requires either legislative expansion of Bolsa Família eligibility or a new automatic top-up instrument with a fiscal rule attached.
  • An RAIS–CadÚnico auto-trigger for early Seguro Desemprego eligibility assessment requires an inter-ministerial data-sharing protocol and budget authorisation.
  • Full platform-worker coverage requires passage of PLP 12/2024 or PLP 152/2025; both remain pending in committee as of April 2026.

02AdaptiveTransitional Risks
Core Risk — No Mid-Career Pathway Outside Banking
An assembled, AI-shock-tailored mid-career transition pathway is not yet visible at scale
Three transitional risks dominate. Skills requirements in AI-exposed occupations are shifting faster than training institutions were designed to absorb — workers in the top quartile of AI exposure exhibit 135% higher net skill change than those in the bottom quartile (PwC, 2025). Brazil has a baseline data protection safeguard under LGPD Article 20, but no enforceable AI-specific employment rights while PL 2338/2023 remains pending. Workers displaced from clerical and administrative roles have no general pathway into AI-complementary jobs: Seguro Desemprego provides income but not transition support, and Sistema S mainly serves youth and incumbent formal workers. The banking CCT with ~3,100 scholarships is the only realised model — its replicability depends on union capacity that is strong in banking and weak elsewhere.
What already exists
Sistema S▾
Employer-funded vocational training network (SENAI for industry, SENAC for commerce) with nationwide physical reach. Financed through payroll-linked employer contributions. Its mandate serves youth and incumbent formal workers; mid-career transition for AI-displaced workers is not a standing part of its remit.
PBIA (2024–2028)▾
Brazil's national AI plan committing up to R$23 billion across five axes including literacy, technical training, graduate pathways, research, and compute infrastructure. Focused on building AI-capable workers — a supply-side plan. It does not specify a delivery mechanism for mid-career retraining of displaced workers.
Mulheres na Tecnologia▾
Retraining scholarships under the 2024–2026 banking CCT: ~3,000 introductory programming/data-skills places through PrograMaria, and 100 advanced-training places through Laboratória. Priority for Black women, single mothers, and transgender women. Limited to formal banking workers with union representation.
ANPD Regulatory Sandbox▾
ANPD's experimental mechanism for supervised testing and regulatory learning in AI and data protection. An entry point for piloting transparency and accountability practices in high-data sectors — not a substitute for enforceable worker rights, but useful while PL 2338/2023 remains pending.
Policy priorities
  • Extend the banking CCT retraining model to other high-AI-exposure sectors through union-level negotiations — where sectoral union capacity exists.
  • Activate PBIA resources and Sistema S delivery capacity for AI-complementary curriculum development targeting mid-career displaced workers, not only new entrants.
  • Use ANPD's regulatory sandbox to pilot transparency and accountability practices while broader governance legislation remains pending.
  • A proactive worker-targeting system using RAIS and CadÚnico requires an inter-ministerial protocol, legal basis for prospective use of identified records, and dedicated funding.
  • Enforceable AI rights in employment require passage and implementation of PL 2338/2023 (pending in the Chamber as of April 2026).
Key terms
Sistema S PBIA CCT (Banking) Mulheres na Tecnologia ANPD RAIS PL 2338/2023
03TransformativeStructural Risks
Core Risk — Payroll-Financed Welfare in a Capital-Intensive AI Economy
Brazil's welfare system is financed through formal employment — as AI shifts gains toward capital, the revenue base erodes
Two structural risks dominate. First, Brazil still finances much of its welfare system through payroll-linked formal employment even as the economy moves toward greater capital intensity and AI-enabled productivity gains accrue disproportionately to capital owners. The VAT reform (test 2026; CBS effective 2027; IBS by 2033 at ~28%) and a 10% dividend withholding tax above BRL 50,000/month (Law 15.270/2025, effective January 2026) are partial steps. Neither creates a dedicated welfare-financing channel. Second, AI investment is clustering in São Paulo (31.1% of national GDP vs. 0.2% for Acre), with data-center expansion occurring without welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms.
What already exists
VAT Reform (CBS & IBS)▾
CBS (federal) and IBS (state/municipal) replace a fragmented indirect tax system. EC 132/2023 initiated the redesign; LC 214/2025 established the new taxes. Test period 2026; CBS effective 2027; full IBS transition by 2033 at ~28%. Broadens the non-payroll tax base but the long phase-in limits the near-term fiscal dividend.
Law 15.270/2025 (Dividend Tax)▾
10% withholding tax on dividends above BRL 50,000/month paid by the same company to the same individual, effective January 2026. Does not cover retained earnings reinvested in companies. A partial step toward taxing capital income — scope and rate are modest relative to the structural challenge.
eSocial + EFD-Reinf▾
Unified employer reporting systems consolidating labour, payroll, social security, FGTS, and non-payroll fiscal information. The administrative data exists to track AI-linked shifts in jobs, taxable activity, and household vulnerability by territory — but no recurring publication yet uses them for this purpose.
Policy priorities
  • Use eSocial, EFD-Reinf, and CadÚnico monitoring tools to publish a recurring territorial assessment of AI-linked shifts in jobs, taxable activity, and household vulnerability — making structural risk visible before it becomes acute.
  • Earmarking dividend-tax or VAT revenues to a dedicated welfare fund requires an additional political commitment — the current measures do not create this channel automatically.
  • Brazil's data-centre framework does not yet create welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms tied to AI infrastructure expansion.
  • An alternative welfare-financing architecture capable of replacing formal-labour contributions at scale requires sustained political commitment to design and legislate.
Key terms
VAT Reform (CBS & IBS) Law 15.270/2025 eSocial EFD-Reinf RAIS CadÚnico
04SystemicAccess & Delivery Risks
Core Risk — INSS Automation Without Governance
Automated benefit analysis has expanded faster than oversight and correction mechanisms have kept pace
Two access-and-delivery risks dominate. First, INSS automated the denial share of decisions from 41% (2021) to 65% (2022), while a 2023 CGU audit identified planning, governance, monitoring, and transparency weaknesses — without triggering standing reform. The CNIS records underlying these decisions contain 24.3 million entries with incomplete, invalid, or inconsistent data (Nicolas and Sampaio, 2024). Second, the CRPS averages 278 days to process an appeal (2025), and Meu INSS creates access barriers for rural, elderly, and digitally constrained claimants who cannot navigate it independently.
What already exists
INSS + Isaac + Dataprev▾
INSS administers pensions, disability, maternity, and sickness benefits. Isaac is the AI-assisted automation layer cross-referencing applicant data against CNIS; Dataprev is the state-owned IT company that operates it. Dataprev faces a structural conflict of interest: the same entity handles both operational delivery and the technical infrastructure for scrutiny.
CRPS (Appeals Board)▾
The Social Security Appeals Board hears appeals against INSS denials. Average processing time is 278 days (2025), and navigation burden is high. Formal appeal rights do not translate into timely redress without binding time targets and published performance data.
CGU + TCU▾
The Comptroller General (CGU) conducts internal audits — its 2023 review identified governance and transparency weaknesses in INSS automation. The Federal Court of Audit (TCU) provides external oversight and has reviewed CNIS data quality. Both operate episodically; no standing routine audit currently exists.
Caixa + LGPD Art. 20▾
Caixa has 4,200+ branches, 9,000+ CAIXA Aqui correspondents, and 13,000+ lottery outlets — already an informal fallback for claimants excluded from digital channels. Not currently listed as an official CRPS intake point. LGPD Article 20 provides a legal right to request human review of automated decisions, but this is case-by-case, not a system-level architecture.
Policy priorities
  • Place automated INSS decision-making under routine CGU audit with mandatory demographic disaggregation of denial rates — published quarterly.
  • Designate agricultural and non-standard claims (segurado especial, irregular contribution histories) as default human-review categories.
  • Set a binding maximum CRPS appeal processing time with published quarterly performance data on caseload, backlog, and staffing ratios.
  • Formalize Caixa branches as official INSS appeal intake and biometric verification points — formalising a role Caixa already plays informally.
  • Enforceable rights to human review and written explanation for automated welfare denials require PL 2338/2023 to pass — LGPD Article 20 provides only a case-by-case baseline.
  • Resolving the Dataprev conflict of interest requires either a legislative mandate for audit independence or an executive restructuring decision.
Key terms
INSS Isaac Dataprev CNIS Meu INSS CRPS CGU TCU Caixa Econômica Federal LGPD ANPD PL 2338/2023
Diagnosis & Conclusion

Overall welfare risk profile — Brazil

Most pressing risk
Adaptive — transitional displacement
Banking is one realised sectoral pressure; broader vulnerability extends across routine clerical, administrative, and office-intensive work, especially for women in high-AI-exposure occupations. The income support system still leaves a large middle group without adequate protection once existing instruments run out.
Strongest capacity dimension
Coping — delivery infrastructure
Pix, CadÚnico, and Bolsa Família together provide a delivery and targeting infrastructure that few comparable economies have. Auxílio Emergencial showed this system can move cash at population scale when political authorisation exists.
Most fragile capacity dimension
Adaptive — no assembled transition system
The banking sector collective agreement is exceptional. Seguro Desemprego provides income but not transition support. Sistema S mainly serves youth and incumbent workers. There is no rules-based connection linking income support, training finance, and placement for AI-displaced workers.
Key data gap
The missing middle is not monitored
No routine monitoring captures displaced workers above the Bolsa Família threshold. Converging proxies — 5.2 million workers at peak AI-exposure risk, 8.4 million formal workers in CadÚnico without active Bolsa Família — suggest millions are affected, but a precise count does not exist.
Four binding constraints across all risk dimensions
01
Automated INSS decisions still lack routine demographic monitoring and publishable denial disaggregation, despite CGU (2023) identifying governance and transparency weaknesses. The 278-day appeal wait means formal redress rights do not function in practice for most claimants.
02
No institutional mechanism connects RAIS, CadÚnico, and Sistema S into a proactive retraining pathway for displaced workers — even though the technical foundations exist.
03
The political commitment to earmark broader tax-base gains toward welfare financing has not been established. Welfare financed through payroll in an increasingly capital-intensive economy faces a structural revenue problem that is not self-resolving.
04
Workers in AI-managed employment still lack enforceable procedural rights — rights to explanation, contestation, and human review. These depend on passage and implementation of PL 2338/2023 or an equivalent framework.
Strategic Sequencing
Near-term
Governance strengthening: routine CGU audit of INSS automation with demographic disaggregation, binding CRPS processing-time targets, default human-review categories for non-standard claims, and Caixa formalised as official intake channel.
Medium-term
Build connective tissue for worker transition: inter-ministerial RAIS–CadÚnico data-sharing protocol, broader Sistema S mandate for mid-career retraining, and extension of the banking CCT model where union capacity permits.
Longer-term
Resolve payroll dependency by directing part of the broader revenue base opened by VAT reform and dividend taxation toward welfare financing — requires sustained political commitment and legislative action.

01CopingDisruptive Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's welfare system already operates in a highly volatile labour market — AI-driven disruptions will compound existing coping gaps
Most workers earn income through informal trade, smallholder agriculture, casual services, and self-employment. Earnings fluctuate frequently, and administrative systems rarely observe these changes in real time. AI is expanding digital labour markets, increasing automation of routine service tasks, and intensifying global competition in digitally traded services. Kenya has some institutional capacity to respond — Inua Jamii provides regular support and mobile money enables large-scale digital transfers — but the system stabilises only part of the labour market. Informal workers outside social assistance eligibility remain exposed to income shocks.
What already exists
Inua Jamii Programme▾
Kenya's social assistance umbrella covering the Older Persons Cash Transfer, Persons with Severe Disabilities Cash Transfer, and Orphans and Vulnerable Children Cash Transfer. Reaches approximately 5.7 million beneficiaries. Provides a regular income floor but eligibility is categorical — it does not function as a broad unemployment buffer.
M-Pesa & Mobile Money▾
Kenya's mobile money infrastructure reaches ~74% of adults with active accounts. Demonstrated scale-up capacity during COVID-19, when waived transaction fees and direct M-Pesa government transfers reached millions quickly. A powerful delivery layer, but not a standing welfare instrument on its own.
Hunger Safety Net Programme▾
Drought-responsive cash transfers in arid and semi-arid counties, scaled up when county-level food insecurity indicators cross thresholds. Kenya's closest existing model for rules-based automatic scale-up. Its limitation is geographic: it is not calibrated for gradual service-sector income shocks that AI-related displacement is more likely to produce.
NSSF▾
Contributory savings scheme covering formal employees. Provides lump-sum benefits on retirement, death, or emigration. Coverage excludes informal workers; contribution levels and benefit adequacy are limited. Does not function as unemployment insurance.
Policy priorities
  • Use mobile money platforms for rapid transfer scale-up to households showing income-shock signals from administrative data — building on the COVID-19 precedent.
  • Strengthen integration between county-level drought response and national social registry targeting, so AI-linked informal-sector disruptions can be identified and responded to.
  • A standing automatic income-stabiliser for informal workers requires a national social registry with sufficient informal-sector coverage and a legal basis for automatic eligibility triggers.
  • Extending contributory unemployment protection beyond the formal sector requires NSSF reform and a viable contribution mechanism for gig and platform workers.

02AdaptiveTransitional Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's training institutions were not designed to absorb mid-career AI-driven transitions at scale
No general pathway exists for mid-career workers displaced by AI into AI-complementary jobs. The TVET system mainly serves youth. Skills mismatches are pronounced: high-AI-exposure occupations require digital literacy and data skills underrepresented in the current working-age population. Workers in digitally mediated and outsourcing activities face increasingly fragmented contracts with no transition support attached.
What already exists
TVET Authority▾
The Technical and Vocational Education and Training Authority coordinates Kenya's vocational training system across ~2,300 institutions nationwide. Primary focus is on youth and pre-employment training; mid-career upskilling for displaced adults is not the standing mandate.
Ajira Digital Programme▾
Government initiative connecting Kenyans to online freelancing and digital work opportunities, with training in digital skills. Demonstrates demand for digital-economy access; but scope is primarily youth-focused and does not yet operate at a scale sufficient to absorb mass AI-driven transitions.
Policy priorities
  • Expand Ajira Digital's reach to mid-career workers and integrate with social registry targeting to identify AI-displaced populations for priority outreach.
  • Use TVET Authority to develop and pilot AI-complementary curriculum modules — data literacy, digital service delivery, AI-assisted productivity — for adult learners in high-exposure sectors.
  • A proactive mid-career transition system requires employer data linkage, a national skills registry, and a funding mechanism — none of which currently exist at sufficient scale.
  • Enforceable AI rights in employment contexts require a national AI governance framework that is still under development.
Key terms
Ajira Digital TVET Authority NEA KLMIS
03TransformativeStructural Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's welfare financing depends on a narrow formal-sector tax base that AI-driven informalization will further erode
Kenya's fiscal capacity for welfare is constrained by a narrow formal-sector revenue base. AI investment is likely to deepen regional concentration around Nairobi, widening already large urban-rural welfare gaps. The emerging digital-economy framework does not yet create welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms tied to AI infrastructure growth. Long-run structural transformation — including the shift toward capital-intensive AI production — poses challenges to a tax system still heavily reliant on formal-sector payroll and consumption taxes.
What already exists
Digital Service Tax▾
1.5% tax on gross transaction value of digital marketplace services provided by non-residents. Applies to streaming, ride-hailing, and online marketplaces. Does not capture profits from AI infrastructure or broader capital income from AI-led productivity gains.
Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF)▾
Health coverage reform replacing NHIF; designed to extend health protection to informal households through revised contribution and registration arrangements. Kenya's most significant recent expansion of social protection beyond the formal sector.
Policy priorities
  • Publish an annual territorial assessment of AI-linked shifts in taxable activity, formal employment, and household vulnerability — making structural risk visible before it becomes acute.
  • Expanding the welfare revenue base to capture AI-linked capital income gains requires tax reform and political commitment that does not currently exist.
  • Tying AI infrastructure investments to local welfare contributions requires a regulatory framework that is not yet in place.
Key terms
SHIF KES 30,000 Exemption National AI Strategy Maisha Namba
04SystemicAccess & Delivery Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's administrative systems lack the beneficiary visibility and data quality needed to respond proactively to AI-driven displacement
Kenya's social assistance eligibility determination relies on categorical targeting with limited dynamic updating. The single registry is not connected to labour-market administrative data in ways that would allow proactive identification of AI-displaced workers. Mobile money provides a powerful delivery channel, but identifying who needs support — rather than delivering it once targeted — is the binding constraint. Digital exclusion remains significant in rural and pastoralist areas.
What already exists
Enhanced Single Registry (ESR)▾
Kenya's integrated beneficiary registry coordinating household information across social protection programmes. Updates infrequently — meaning household circumstances can change substantially before the registry reflects them. Interoperability with other administrative systems remains incomplete.
Maisha Namba▾
Kenya's new national digital identity system with a Unique Personal Identifier. As digital identity becomes the gateway to welfare services, enrolment gaps risk excluding marginalised populations — particularly those with documentation barriers or in remote areas.
Kenya Data Protection Act (2019)▾
Kenya's data protection legislation establishing the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner. Provides a legal basis for regulating data use in AI-driven administrative decisions, but enforcement capacity and AI-specific guidance are still developing.
Policy priorities
  • Integrate mobile money transaction data with the single registry on an opt-in basis to improve dynamic beneficiary identification and income-shock detection.
  • Publish guidance on how the Data Protection Act applies to algorithmic decision-making in social protection contexts.
  • A proactive, real-time targeting system for AI-displaced workers requires labour-market administrative data linkage and a legal basis for prospective use of individual records.
  • Enforceable rights to human review and explanation for automated welfare decisions require AI-specific regulation that is not yet enacted.

Diagnosis & Conclusion

Overall welfare risk profile — Kenya

Most pressing risk
Systemic — administrative visibility gap
Kenya's delivery infrastructure (mobile money, county networks) is strong, but identifying who needs support is the binding constraint. The single registry lacks coverage of the informal sector and is not connected to labour-market data in ways that would enable proactive responses to AI-driven displacement.
Strongest capacity dimension
Coping — mobile delivery infrastructure
M-Pesa and Kenya's mobile money ecosystem provide delivery reach that few lower-middle-income economies can match. COVID-19 demonstrated the system can move cash to millions rapidly. The challenge is connecting this delivery capacity to an identification and targeting system that responds to AI-linked disruptions.
Most fragile capacity dimension
Systemic — enrolment, registry, and data systems
The most fragile capacity lies in enrolment processes, registry maintenance, and labour market data visibility. Social registries are updated infrequently, identity systems face enrolment gaps, and official statistics do not capture platform and informal earnings. As digital administration expands, these gaps translate directly into exclusion.
Key data gap
Informal-sector AI exposure is unmeasured
With 83% informality, Kenya lacks the administrative data to assess how AI is affecting the distribution of work, income, and vulnerability across the majority of its workforce. Without this visibility, both policy design and targeting are necessarily reactive rather than proactive.
Strategic Sequencing
Near-term
Improve administrative visibility: opt-in mobile money data integration with the single registry, guidance on Data Protection Act application to welfare AI systems, and a territorial assessment of AI-linked labour-market shifts. Achievable within existing institutional authority.
Medium-term
Build targeting and retraining connective tissue: labour-market data linkage to the single registry, expansion of TVET and Ajira Digital to mid-career displaced workers, and an AI governance framework that includes worker rights protections.
Longer-term
Resolve the revenue-base constraint by expanding welfare financing beyond formal-sector payroll — requires sustained political commitment to digital economy taxation and welfare financing reform.
