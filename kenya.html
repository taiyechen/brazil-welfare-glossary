The Welfare Resilience Framework

Four dimensions of institutional capacity

The framework links three elements in sequence: economic risks created by technological change, policy instruments intended to address those risks, and the enabling infrastructure required to deliver those policies effectively. Its central premise is that policy ambition must be matched by institutional capacity.

Disruptive Risk
Short-term shocks such as sudden income loss, unstable hours, or rapid firm-level restructuring. These test a system's coping capacity, meaning its ability to stabilise households quickly and prevent hardship when labour market conditions shift abruptly.
Transitional Risks
Risks that arise over the medium term as tasks, occupations and sectors reconfigure. These require adaptive capacity: institutions capable of supporting worker mobility, re-skilling and reallocation as the structure of employment evolves.
Structural Risks
Risks that emerge gradually through long-run shifts in the relationship between labour, wages and productivity. These test transformative capacity, whether fiscal and welfare institutions are deep enough to manage persistent inequality, regional divergence or changes in how economic value is distributed.
Access & Delivery Risks
Systemic capacity which includes the administrative, legal, fiscal and digital systems that allow policies to function in practice. The framework emphasises that even well-designed policies will fail if these enabling conditions are weak.

01 Coping Disruptive Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's welfare system already operates in a highly volatile labour market — AI-driven disruptions will compound existing coping gaps
Most workers earn income through informal trade, smallholder agriculture, casual services, and self-employment. Earnings fluctuate frequently, and administrative systems rarely observe these changes in real time. AI is expanding digital labour markets, increasing automation of routine service tasks, and intensifying global competition in digitally traded services. Kenya has some institutional capacity to respond to income shocks — Inua Jamii provides regular support to vulnerable households, and mobile money enables large-scale digital payments — but the system stabilises only part of the labour market.
What already exists
Inua Jamii Programme▾
Kenya's social assistance umbrella covering the Older Persons Cash Transfer, Persons with Severe Disabilities Cash Transfer, and Orphans and Vulnerable Children Cash Transfer. Reaches approximately 5.7 million beneficiaries. Provides a regular income floor but eligibility is categorical — it does not function as a broad unemployment buffer.
M-Pesa & Mobile Money▾
Kenya's mobile money infrastructure reaches ~74% of adults with active accounts. Demonstrated scale-up capacity during COVID-19, when waived transaction fees and direct M-Pesa government transfers reached millions quickly. A powerful delivery layer, but not a standing welfare instrument on its own.
Hunger Safety Net Programme▾
Drought-responsive cash transfers in arid and semi-arid counties, scaled up as county-level food insecurity indicators cross thresholds. Demonstrates responsive design, but geographic scope is limited to climate-stressed counties — not a general income-shock response mechanism.
NSSF (National Social Security Fund)▾
Contributory savings scheme covering formal employees. Provides lump-sum benefits on retirement, death, or emigration. Coverage excludes informal workers; contribution levels and benefit adequacy are limited. Does not function as unemployment insurance.
Policy priorities
  • Use mobile money platforms for rapid transfer scale-up to households showing income-shock signals from administrative data — building on the COVID-19 precedent.
  • Strengthen integration between county-level drought response and national social registry targeting, so AI-linked informal-sector disruptions can be identified and responded to.
  • A standing automatic income-stabiliser for informal workers requires a national social registry with sufficient informal-sector coverage and a legal basis for automatic eligibility triggers.
  • Extending contributory unemployment protection beyond the formal sector requires NSSF reform and a viable contribution mechanism for gig and platform workers.

02 Adaptive Transitional Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's training institutions were not designed to absorb mid-career AI-driven transitions at scale
Kenya's main disruptive risks arise from multiple sources of income volatility, including informal workers without income protection, climate-exposed rural households, young workers in unstable service sectors, and workers in digitally mediated and outsourcing activities where contracts are increasingly fragmented. No general pathway exists for mid-career workers displaced by AI into AI-complementary jobs. The TVET system mainly serves youth. Skills mismatches are pronounced: high-AI-exposure occupations require digital literacy and data skills that are underrepresented in the current working-age population.
What already exists
TVET Authority▾
The Technical and Vocational Education and Training Authority coordinates Kenya's vocational training system. Over 1,000 accredited institutions nationwide. Primary focus is on youth and pre-employment training; mid-career upskilling for displaced adults is not the standing mandate.
Ajira Digital Programme▾
Government initiative to connect young Kenyans to online freelancing and digital work opportunities, with training in digital skills. Demonstrates demand for digital-economy access; but scope is primarily youth-focused and does not yet operate at a scale sufficient to absorb mass AI-driven transitions.
Konza Technopolis / Digital Economy▾
Kenya's flagship digital economy infrastructure investment, positioned to attract BPO, IT, and AI-adjacent industry. Creates some demand for digital skills but does not directly deliver retraining capacity for currently displaced workers.
Policy priorities
  • Expand Ajira Digital's reach to mid-career workers and integrate with social registry targeting to identify AI-displaced populations for priority outreach.
  • Use TVET Authority to develop and pilot AI-complementary curriculum modules — data literacy, digital service delivery, AI-assisted productivity — for adult learners in high-exposure sectors.
  • A proactive mid-career transition system requires employer data linkage, a national skills registry, and a funding mechanism — none of which currently exist at sufficient scale.
  • Enforceable AI rights in employment contexts require a national AI governance framework that is still under development.

Ajira Digital TVET Authority NEA KLMIS
03 Transformative Structural Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's welfare financing depends on a narrow formal-sector tax base that AI-driven informalization will further erode
Kenya's fiscal capacity for welfare is constrained by a narrow formal-sector revenue base. AI investment is likely to deepen regional concentration around Nairobi, widening already large urban-rural welfare gaps. The emerging digital-economy framework does not yet create welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms tied to AI infrastructure growth. Long-run structural transformation — including the shift toward capital-intensive AI production — poses challenges to a tax system still heavily reliant on formal-sector payroll and consumption taxes.
What already exists
Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA)▾
KRA administers tax collection and has been expanding digital tax compliance infrastructure. Digital service taxes have been introduced for non-resident digital service providers. A partial step toward capturing value from the digital economy, but the rate and coverage remain limited.
Digital Service Tax▾
1.5% tax on gross transaction value of digital marketplace services provided or facilitated by non-residents. Applies to streaming, ride-hailing, and online marketplaces. Does not capture profits from AI infrastructure or broader capital income from AI-led productivity gains.
Policy priorities
  • Publish an annual territorial assessment of AI-linked shifts in taxable activity, formal employment, and household vulnerability — making structural risk visible before it becomes acute.
  • Expanding the welfare revenue base to capture AI-linked capital income gains requires tax reform and political commitment that does not currently exist.
  • Tying AI infrastructure investments (data centres, cloud providers) to local welfare contributions requires a regulatory framework that is not yet in place.

04 Systemic Access & Delivery Risks
Core Risk
Kenya's administrative systems lack the beneficiary visibility and data quality needed to respond proactively to AI-driven displacement
Kenya's social assistance eligibility determination relies on categorical targeting with limited dynamic updating. The single registry is not connected to labour-market administrative data in ways that would allow proactive identification of AI-displaced workers. Mobile money provides a powerful delivery channel, but identifying who needs support — rather than delivering it once targeted — is the binding constraint. Digital exclusion remains significant in rural and pastoralist areas.
What already exists
Single Registry (NSSF / NASSEP)▾
Kenya's social protection administrative registry, covering beneficiaries of Inua Jamii and other programmes. Not connected to labour-market administrative data. Coverage of the informal sector is limited; proactive targeting of newly displaced workers is not within the system's current design.
Huduma Namba (National ID)▾
Kenya's national biometric identity system, intended to serve as a foundation for service delivery and data linkage across government. Implementation has faced legal and technical challenges. Where functional, it provides a potential anchor for social protection data integration.
Kenya Data Protection Act (2019)▾
Kenya's data protection legislation, establishing the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner. Provides a legal basis for regulating data use in AI-driven administrative decisions, but enforcement capacity and AI-specific guidance are still developing.
Policy priorities
  • Integrate mobile money transaction data with the single registry on an opt-in basis to improve dynamic beneficiary identification and income-shock detection.
  • Publish guidance on how the Data Protection Act applies to algorithmic decision-making in social protection contexts, building on the Office of the Data Protection Commissioner's mandate.
  • A proactive, real-time targeting system for AI-displaced workers requires labour-market administrative data linkage and a legal basis for prospective use of individual records.
  • Enforceable rights to human review and explanation for automated welfare decisions require AI-specific regulation that is not yet enacted.

Diagnosis & Conclusion

Overall welfare risk profile

Most pressing risk
Systemic — administrative visibility gap
Kenya's delivery infrastructure (mobile money, county networks) is strong, but identifying who needs support is the binding constraint. The single registry lacks coverage of the informal sector and is not connected to labour-market data in ways that would enable proactive responses to AI-driven displacement.
Strongest capacity dimension
Coping — mobile delivery infrastructure
M-Pesa and Kenya's mobile money ecosystem provide delivery reach that few lower-middle-income economies can match. COVID-19 demonstrated the system can move cash to millions rapidly. The challenge is connecting this delivery capacity to an identification and targeting system that can respond to AI-linked disruptions.
Most fragile capacity dimension
Transformative — revenue base
Kenya's fiscal capacity for welfare is constrained and vulnerable to AI-driven formality erosion. The digital service tax is a beginning, but capturing value from AI-linked capital income at meaningful scale requires deeper tax reform and political commitment not currently in evidence.
Key data gap
Informal-sector AI exposure is unmeasured
With 83% informality, Kenya lacks the administrative data to assess how AI is affecting the distribution of work, income, and vulnerability across the majority of its workforce. Without this visibility, both policy design and targeting are necessarily reactive rather than proactive.
Strategic Sequencing
Near-term
Improve administrative visibility: opt-in mobile money data integration with the single registry, guidance on Data Protection Act application to welfare AI systems, and a territorial assessment of AI-linked labour-market shifts. Achievable within existing institutional authority.
Medium-term
Build the targeting and retraining connective tissue: labour-market data linkage to the single registry, expansion of TVET and Ajira Digital to mid-career displaced workers, and an AI governance framework that includes worker rights protections.
Longer-term
Resolve the revenue-base constraint by expanding welfare financing beyond formal-sector payroll — requires sustained political commitment to digital economy taxation and welfare financing reform.
