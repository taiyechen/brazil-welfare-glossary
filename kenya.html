Inua Jamii Cash Transfer Programme
Social Program
Kenya's flagship multi-component cash transfer covering older persons, persons with severe disabilities, and orphans and vulnerable children — the primary income floor for the most vulnerable households.
The Inua Jamii programme is Kenya's main social assistance initiative, administered by the Ministry of Labour and Social Protection. It comprises three sub-programmes: the Older Persons Cash Transfer (OPCT), the Cash Transfer for Persons with Severe Disabilities (PWSD-CT), and the Cash Transfer for Orphans and Vulnerable Children (CT-OVC). In this paper it is the primary income floor for households outside the labour market and a key mechanism for weathering income shocks. Coverage remains limited relative to the scale of vulnerability — it does not reach informal workers above its means threshold who fall outside the programme's target categories — and benefit levels are modest relative to the poverty line.
Coping Cross-cutting
Hunger Safety Net Programme (HSNP)
Social Program
Shock-responsive cash transfer in arid and semi-arid counties, linked to drought early-warning triggers — Kenya's closest existing model for rules-based automatic scale-up.
A government programme supported by development partners, providing regular and shock-responsive cash transfers to households in Kenya's arid and semi-arid land (ASAL) counties. It is explicitly designed to scale up automatically when NDMA early-warning drought triggers are met — making it the most developed example of a rules-based automatic stabiliser in Kenya's welfare architecture. In this paper it matters as both an operational safety net and a proof of concept for extending trigger-based responses from climate shocks to economic and labour market shocks. Its limitation is geographic: it reaches only ASAL counties and is not calibrated for the gradual service-sector income shocks that AI-related displacement is more likely to produce.

Kazi Mtaani
Social Program
Government public works programme providing temporary paid employment to urban youth; reached approximately 300,000 beneficiaries during the COVID-19 pandemic.
A government-administered public employment programme that provided temporary income support through short-term work assignments, primarily to urban youth. During the COVID-19 pandemic it reached approximately 300,000 beneficiaries, demonstrating Kenya's ability to mobilise emergency employment at meaningful scale. In this paper it matters as evidence of emergency deployment capacity. Its limitations for AI-related labour market change are also clear: the programme is urban-focused, temporary by design, and administratively intensive — it addresses acute discrete shocks rather than the gradual structural displacement that AI is more likely to produce across service sectors.

Ajira Digital Programme
Social Program
Government digital skills and online work access programme targeting youth and job seekers; Kenya's main policy instrument for entry into digital labour markets.
A government programme providing digital skills training and access to online work platforms, primarily targeting youth and job seekers. It aims to expand participation in online labour markets and build foundational digital competencies. In this paper it is Kenya's main explicit policy response to the shift toward digital and platform work — establishing institutional responsibility for digital employment and creating entry points into online labour markets. Its limitations for AI-related transition are significant: there is limited evidence on sustained earnings outcomes, no systematic progression pathway from entry-level tasks to higher-skill roles, and no mechanism addressing income instability or career development for workers already working on platforms.
Adaptive
Social Health Insurance Fund (SHIF)
Social Program
Health coverage reform replacing the National Hospital Insurance Fund (NHIF); designed to extend health protection to informal households through revised contribution and registration arrangements.
A major structural reform to Kenya's health coverage system, replacing the National Hospital Insurance Fund (NHIF) with a redesigned fund intended to extend health protection beyond formal employment. The SHIF aims to reach informal and lower-income households through revised contribution models and registration pathways. In this paper it represents Kenya's most significant recent expansion of social protection beyond the formal sector — widening the pool of households covered by a major welfare benefit. Its relevance to AI transition is primarily structural: extending health coverage reduces one of the most significant vulnerabilities facing informal workers who lack employer-provided insurance, helping anchor household resilience during periods of labour market disruption.

Enhanced Single Registry (ESR)
Data System
Integrated beneficiary database coordinating household and eligibility data across Kenya's social protection programmes; the central targeting infrastructure — but updates infrequently and lacks full interoperability.
Kenya's integrated beneficiary registry, coordinating household information across multiple social protection programmes and managed by the Ministry of Labour and Social Protection. It provides a foundation for harmonised targeting and programme coordination. In this paper it is both the central data infrastructure for scaling responses to shocks and a binding constraint on access and delivery: the ESR updates infrequently, meaning household circumstances can change substantially before the registry reflects them. Interoperability with other administrative systems — digital identity, financial registries, labour data — remains incomplete, limiting the potential for automated, accurate eligibility determination.
Systemic Cross-cutting
Kenya Labour Market Information System (KLMIS)
Data System
National labour market data system operated by the National Employment Authority; covers formal employment but has limited visibility of platform, gig, and informal work where AI exposure is greatest.
Kenya's primary national labour market data system, operated by the National Employment Authority (NEA). It centralises labour market statistics and supports employment service administration and job matching. In this paper it represents an important but incomplete data asset: its current coverage of informal, casual, and digital platform work is limited, creating a gap in the ability to monitor exactly the sectors where AI-related disruption is expanding fastest. Extending KLMIS to capture platform work participation, contract duration, and earnings volatility is a key enabling condition for both adaptive policy design and proactive identification of at-risk workers.
Adaptive Systemic
NDMA Drought Early Warning System
Data System
High-frequency climate and food security monitoring system for ASAL counties; generates indicators that automatically trigger HSNP emergency scale-up — Kenya's template for data-driven automatic stabilisers.
A monitoring system operated by the National Drought Management Authority (NDMA) that tracks climate, food security, and livelihood conditions across Kenya's arid and semi-arid land counties. It generates regular indicators that can trigger automatic scale-up of the Hunger Safety Net Programme, creating a functioning link between observed shocks and welfare response. In this paper it matters as the most developed example of a rules-based, data-driven trigger mechanism in Kenya's welfare architecture — providing a model for designing analogous mechanisms linked to labour market data rather than climate indicators. The main challenge is that the underlying data infrastructure for economic shocks does not yet exist at comparable frequency or resolution.
Coping
National Employment Authority (NEA)
Institution
Government agency managing Kenya's public employment services and operating KLMIS; the institutional centre for job matching and labour market transition support.
The government agency responsible for public employment services in Kenya, including operation of the Kenya Labour Market Information System (KLMIS) and delivery of job-matching and transition support services. In this paper it is the core institutional actor for managing labour market transitions. Its current limitations include limited integration with private employers and digital labour platforms, and weak coverage of informal and platform work within its data and service systems. Strengthening NEA's capacity to track and serve workers in digital and AI-exposed occupations is central to improving Kenya's adaptive capacity for the AI transition.
Adaptive
TVET System / TVET Authority
Institution
Kenya's technical and vocational training network of approximately 2,300 institutions; the main skills pathway for the workforce, but curriculum updates are slow relative to the pace of AI-driven changes in labour demand.
Kenya's Technical and Vocational Education and Training system, governed by the TVET Authority under the State Department for TVET. It comprises approximately 2,300 institutions nationwide, representing the largest formal training infrastructure in the country. In this paper it is the primary vehicle for skills development and workforce transition — but it faces fundamental alignment constraints: the curriculum update cycle is slow, digital and AI-adjacent content is uneven across institutions, and quality varies substantially between urban and rural locations. Aligning TVET content with AI-related labour demand — including supervisor-level and complementary skills rather than routine task training — is the central transition challenge in Kenya's adaptive capacity.
Adaptive Cross-cutting
National Drought Management Authority (NDMA)
Institution
Government authority managing drought risk and emergency response in ASAL counties; operates the early warning system that triggers HSNP scale-up — Kenya's reference institution for trigger-based welfare response.
A government authority coordinating drought management and response across Kenya's arid and semi-arid land counties. The NDMA operates Kenya's drought early warning system, which tracks climate and food security conditions and generates the indicators that trigger automatic HSNP emergency top-ups. In this paper it matters as the institutional home of Kenya's most developed trigger-based response mechanism — and as a reference point for institutional design of analogous economic shock response systems. The challenge is that economic and labour market shocks require different data inputs and response architectures than climate-driven agricultural shocks.
Coping Systemic
Ministry of Labour and Social Protection
Institution
Central government ministry responsible for social assistance, employment policy, and the Enhanced Single Registry — the primary institutional coordinator of Kenya's welfare response to labour market change.
The central government ministry responsible for social assistance programmes, employment policy, and social protection administration in Kenya. It administers the Inua Jamii programme, oversees the Enhanced Single Registry, coordinates with the National Employment Authority and the TVET Authority, and has lead responsibility for the policy responses considered in this paper. In this paper it functions as the primary institutional actor across all four welfare resilience dimensions — and the ministry through which most near-term and medium-term policy recommendations would need to be channelled to take effect.
Cross-cutting
Central Bank of Kenya
Institution
Monetary authority and regulator of Kenya's payment systems including mobile money; its oversight framework underpins the welfare delivery infrastructure that makes fast, low-cost cash transfers possible.
Kenya's central bank and monetary authority, which regulates the financial system including mobile money operators such as Safaricom (M-Pesa). The Central Bank of Kenya's regulatory oversight shapes the conditions under which government transfers can reach beneficiaries through mobile money at low cost and at scale. In this paper it matters primarily as the institutional foundation for Kenya's mobile money infrastructure — the strongest delivery asset in the welfare system — and as the anchor for Kenya's National Financial Inclusion Strategy 2025–2028.
Systemic
M-Pesa / Mobile Money Infrastructure
Technology
Kenya's dominant mobile money platform and the primary delivery channel for government welfare transfers; over 80% of adults hold mobile money accounts — the strongest delivery asset in Kenya's welfare system.
Kenya's dominant mobile money platform, operated by Safaricom, and the broader mobile money ecosystem surrounding it. More than 80% of Kenyan adults hold mobile money accounts. In this paper it is consistently identified as the most important operational asset in the welfare delivery system: once eligibility is confirmed, transfers can reach most beneficiaries quickly and at low cost. The paper makes a critical distinction between delivery capacity (strong) and identification and targeting capacity (weak): the binding constraints in Kenya's welfare system lie upstream of M-Pesa — in registry quality, enrolment systems, and eligibility determination — not in the payment rail itself.
Coping Systemic Cross-cutting
eCitizen
Technology
Kenya's central digital government platform for public service access — identification, licensing, applications, and payments — the primary digital interface between citizens and the state.
Kenya's unified digital government platform that consolidates access to government services including identification, licensing, applications, and payments. In this paper it matters as part of Kenya's digital public infrastructure for welfare delivery and administrative access. Like mobile money, it reflects Kenya's strength in digital delivery systems — but it assumes internet access, digital literacy, and valid identity documentation, all of which constrain access for the rural and low-income populations most likely to need welfare support during an AI transition. The gap between platform availability and effective access is a recurring theme in the paper's access-and-delivery diagnosis.
Systemic
Maisha Namba
Technology
Kenya's new national digital identity system, replacing the legacy national ID with a Unique Personal Identifier — central to public service access, but enrolment gaps risk excluding marginalised populations from services.
Kenya's new national digital identity system, replacing the legacy national ID card with a unified digital credential linked to a Unique Personal Identifier (UPI). As digital identity becomes the gateway to welfare services, government platforms, and financial products, Maisha Namba's coverage gaps matter directly for social protection access. In this paper it is the centre of a key access-and-delivery risk: populations unable to enrol — due to documentation barriers, geographic remoteness, or registration system failures — face exclusion from welfare services that are increasingly conditioned on digital identity verification. Litigation documented by Namati (2025) highlights that these exclusion risks are not hypothetical.
Transformative Systemic
KES 30,000 Income Tax Exemption
Policy
Fiscal policy removing income tax for formal workers earning below KES 30,000 per month — increases take-home pay for lower-income formal workers but leaves informal earners unaffected.
A budget policy change exempting formal workers earning below KES 30,000 per month from income tax, representing the most recent shift in Kenya's tax-and-redistribution position relevant to structural risks. In this paper it matters as a targeted relief measure for lower-income formal workers. Two limitations frame its significance for the AI transition: it does not reach the more than 80% of workers in informal employment, who face no income tax and gain nothing from the exemption; and it narrows the formal tax base, reducing the fiscal space available for broader redistributive programmes. It illustrates a persistent tension in Kenya's welfare policy between immediate relief for formal workers and long-run fiscal capacity for universal coverage.
Transformative
National AI Strategy 2025–2030
Strategy
Kenya's five-year government AI strategy identifying priority sectors (agriculture, health, finance, public services) and signalling government intent to shape the direction and pace of AI adoption.
Kenya's national AI strategy covering 2025–2030, which identifies priority sectors for AI adoption and development including agriculture, health, finance, and public services. It signals government intent to direct the focus of AI deployment and establishes a framework for cross-government coordination on AI-related policy. In this paper it matters as an enabling condition for long-term structural policy — but it is primarily a statement of direction rather than a funded operational programme. Strategy documents establish priorities and create inter-agency coordination hooks, but do not substitute for institutional capacity, regulatory frameworks, or dedicated fiscal commitments that would be needed to translate the strategy into welfare-relevant outcomes.
Transformative Cross-cutting
Kenya National Financial Inclusion Strategy 2025–2028
Strategy
Multi-year government financial inclusion plan led by the Central Bank of Kenya; sets targets for expanding mobile money, digital accounts, and credit access for underserved populations.
Kenya's multi-year financial inclusion plan, led by the Central Bank of Kenya in coordination with other ministries and agencies. It sets out targets for expanding access to financial services — including mobile money, digital accounts, and credit — for underserved populations including rural and low-income households. In this paper it matters as a coordination framework that underpins the welfare delivery infrastructure: broader financial inclusion reduces the barriers to reaching excluded populations with government transfers and strengthens the foundation on which Kenya's strongest delivery asset — mobile money — rests. Its relevance to AI transition is primarily systemic rather than programmatic.
