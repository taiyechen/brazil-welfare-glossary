The Welfare Resilience Framework

Four dimensions of institutional capacity

The framework links three elements in sequence: economic risks created by technological change, policy instruments intended to address those risks, and the enabling infrastructure required to deliver those policies effectively. Its central premise is that policy ambition must be matched by institutional capacity.

Disruptive Risk
Short-term shocks such as sudden income loss, unstable hours, or rapid firm-level restructuring. These test a system's coping capacity, meaning its ability to stabilise households quickly and prevent hardship when labour market conditions shift abruptly.
Transitional Risks
Risks that arise over the medium term as tasks, occupations and sectors reconfigure. These require adaptive capacity: institutions capable of supporting worker mobility, re-skilling and reallocation as the structure of employment evolves.
Structural Risks
Risks that emerge gradually through long-run shifts in the relationship between labour, wages and productivity. These test transformative capacity, whether fiscal and welfare institutions are deep enough to manage persistent inequality, regional divergence or changes in how economic value is distributed.
Access & Delivery Risks
Systemic capacity which includes the administrative, legal, fiscal and digital systems that allow policies to function in practice. The framework emphasises that even well-designed policies will fail if these enabling conditions are weak.

01 Coping Disruptive Risks
Core Risk — The Missing Middle
Brazil protects the poorest households and formal workers in the short run — but not the gap between them
Three disruptive risks dominate. First, about 40% of Brazil's workforce is informal and ineligible for contributory protections; 1.7 million platform workers are growing rapidly, with 64.1% contributing nothing to social security (IBGE, 2025). Second, banking is the clearest near-term formal-sector case: 7,473 banking jobs eliminated in the 12 months to Q1 2025, with core banking activities down 9,105 while IT rose only 1,842 (Contraf-CUT, 2025). Third, 35.4% of female workers are in AI-exposed occupations versus 25.2% of male workers (Barbosa Filho et al., 2025). No single programme automatically covers displaced formal workers above the Bolsa Família threshold — the "missing middle" numbers in the millions, with 8.4 million formal workers already registered in CadÚnico but no longer receiving Bolsa Família as a proxy for its scale.
What already exists
Bolsa Família + CadÚnico▾
Federal cash transfer reaching 20.8 million families (~55 million people, ~26% of population) via the national social registry of 96.8 million. The Regra de Proteção lets enrolled families keep 50% of the benefit for up to 12 months after income rises above the threshold — tightened in mid-2025 to cap the transition period. A strong floor, but not a rules-based response to routine displacement for workers above the threshold who were never enrolled.
Seguro Desemprego▾
3–5 months of unemployment insurance for formal workers dismissed without cause, at roughly 80% of prior wages, delivered via Caixa. Designed for cyclical unemployment, not structural displacement: the window typically closes before AI-driven re-employment is secured, and informal workers are excluded entirely.
FGTS▾
Mandatory employer-funded severance reserve: 8% of monthly salary deposited into an individual Caixa account, accessible on dismissal without cause. Supplements Seguro Desemprego as a forced savings buffer, but — like Seguro Desemprego — was designed for cyclical unemployment and does not address prolonged structural displacement.
Auxílio Emergencial (2020)▾
Emergency COVID transfer reaching ~66 million beneficiaries within weeks via CadÚnico and Caixa. Proof of concept that rapid, large-scale cash mobilisation is technically feasible — but required extraordinary political authorisation. No standing rule replicates this capacity under normal conditions.
Policy priorities
  • Actively communicate the Regra de Proteção as a limited re-employment bridge for low-income formal workers cycling in and out of Bolsa Família — no new legislation required.
  • Extend the banking-sector CCT retraining model to other high-exposure sectors through union-level negotiations, using existing collective bargaining frameworks. Not directly portable to retail, BPO, and back-office services where union density is much weaker.
  • Operationalize Seguro Desemprego delivery via Caixa branches for populations excluded from digital channels.
  • A standing income bridge for the missing middle requires either legislative expansion of Bolsa Família eligibility or a new automatic top-up instrument with a fiscal rule attached.
  • An RAIS–CadÚnico auto-trigger for early Seguro Desemprego eligibility assessment requires an inter-ministerial data-sharing protocol and budget authorisation.
  • Full platform-worker coverage requires passage of PLP 12/2024 or PLP 152/2025 and credible enforcement against platform reclassification; both remain pending in committee as of April 2026.

02 Adaptive Transitional Risks
Core Risk — No Mid-Career Pathway Outside Banking
An assembled, AI-shock-tailored mid-career transition pathway is not yet visible at scale
Three transitional risks dominate. Skills requirements in AI-exposed occupations are shifting faster than training institutions were designed to absorb — workers in the top quartile of AI exposure exhibit 135% higher net skill change than those in the bottom quartile (PwC, 2025). Brazil has a baseline data protection safeguard under LGPD Article 20, but no enforceable AI-specific employment rights while PL 2338/2023 remains pending. And workers displaced from clerical and administrative roles have no general pathway into AI-complementary jobs: Seguro Desemprego provides income but not transition support, and Sistema S mainly serves youth and incumbent formal workers. The banking CCT with ~3,100 scholarships (3,000 through PrograMaria + 100 through Laboratória) is the only realised model — its replicability depends on union capacity that is strong in banking and weak elsewhere.
What already exists
Sistema S▾
Employer-funded vocational training network (SENAI for industry, SENAC for commerce) with nationwide physical reach and experienced faculty. Financed through payroll-linked employer contributions. Its mandate and curriculum serve youth and incumbent formal workers; mid-career transition for AI-displaced workers is not a standing part of its remit.
PBIA (2024–2028)▾
Brazil's national AI plan committing up to R$23 billion across five axes including literacy, technical training, graduate pathways, research, and compute infrastructure. Focused on building AI-capable workers — a supply-side plan. It does not, on its own, specify a delivery mechanism for mid-career retraining of displaced workers.
Mulheres na Tecnologia▾
Retraining scholarships under the 2024–2026 banking CCT: approximately 3,000 introductory programming/data-skills places through PrograMaria, and 100 advanced-training places through Laboratória. Priority for Black women, single mothers, and transgender women. Limited to formal banking workers with union representation — a bounded template, not a scalable architecture.
ANPD Regulatory Sandbox▾
ANPD's experimental mechanism for supervised testing and regulatory learning in AI and data protection. An entry point for piloting transparency and accountability practices in high-data sectors — not a substitute for enforceable worker rights, but useful in the interim while PL 2338/2023 remains pending.
Policy priorities
  • Extend the banking CCT retraining model to other high-AI-exposure sectors (retail, administrative services, BPO) through union-level negotiations, using existing collective bargaining frameworks — where sectoral union capacity exists.
  • Activate PBIA resources and Sistema S delivery capacity for AI-complementary curriculum development targeting mid-career displaced workers, not only new entrants.
  • Use ANPD's regulatory sandbox to pilot transparency and accountability practices for AI deployment in high-data-exposure sectors while broader governance legislation remains pending.
  • A proactive worker-targeting system using RAIS and CadÚnico requires an inter-ministerial protocol, a clear legal basis for prospective use of identified records, and dedicated funding for follow-through.
  • Enforceable AI rights in employment — including rights to explanation, contestation, and human review for worker-management systems — require passage and implementation of PL 2338/2023 (pending in the Chamber as of April 2026).

03 Transformative Structural Risks
Core Risk — Payroll-Financed Welfare in a Capital-Intensive AI Economy
Brazil's welfare system is financed through formal employment — as AI shifts gains toward capital, the revenue base erodes
Two structural risks dominate. First, Brazil still finances much of its welfare system through payroll-linked formal employment even as the economy moves toward greater capital intensity and AI-enabled productivity gains accrue disproportionately to capital owners. Two recent measures — the VAT reform (test period 2026; CBS effective 2027; full IBS transition by 2033 at a consolidated ~28% rate) and a 10% dividend withholding tax on distributions above BRL 50,000/month (Law 15.270/2025, effective January 2026) — represent partial steps toward broadening the revenue base. Neither creates a dedicated welfare-financing channel. Second, AI investment is clustering in the Southeast, particularly São Paulo (31.1% of national GDP vs. 0.2% for Acre), building on already highly uneven development — with data-center expansion occurring without welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms.
What already exists
VAT Reform (CBS & IBS)▾
Major restructuring of indirect taxation: CBS (federal) and IBS (state/municipal) replace a fragmented system. EC 132/2023 initiated the redesign; LC 214/2025 established the new taxes. Test period runs in 2026; CBS becomes effective 2027; full IBS transition by 2033 with a consolidated rate of ~28%. Broadens the non-payroll tax base but the long phase-in and state-compensation mechanisms limit the near-term fiscal dividend.
Law 15.270/2025 (Dividend Tax)▾
10% withholding tax on dividends above BRL 50,000/month paid by the same company to the same individual, effective January 2026. Applies to both residents and non-residents. Does not cover retained earnings reinvested in companies. A partial step toward taxing capital income — scope and rate are modest relative to the structural challenge.
eSocial + EFD-Reinf▾
Unified employer reporting systems consolidating labour, payroll, social security, FGTS, and non-payroll fiscal information. In this paper they matter as monitoring infrastructure: the administrative data exists to track AI-linked shifts in jobs, taxable activity, and household vulnerability by territory — but no recurring publication yet uses them for this purpose.
Policy priorities
  • Use eSocial, EFD-Reinf, and CadÚnico monitoring tools to publish a recurring territorial assessment of AI-linked shifts in jobs, taxable activity, and household vulnerability — making structural risk visible before it becomes acute.
  • Earmarking dividend-tax or VAT revenues to a dedicated welfare fund requires an additional political commitment — the current measures do not create this channel automatically.
  • Brazil's emerging data-centre framework imposes industrial and sustainability conditionalities but does not yet create welfare-oriented value-capture mechanisms (local training, household support, redistribution) tied to AI infrastructure expansion.
  • An alternative welfare-financing architecture capable of replacing formal-labour contributions at scale does not yet exist and requires sustained political commitment to design and legislate.

04 Systemic Access & Delivery Risks
Core Risk — INSS Automation Without Governance
Automated benefit analysis has expanded faster than oversight and correction mechanisms have kept pace
Two access-and-delivery risks dominate. First, INSS automated the denial share of decisions from 41% (2021) to 65% (2022), while a 2023 CGU audit identified planning, governance, monitoring, and transparency weaknesses — without triggering standing reform. The CNIS records underlying these decisions contain 24.3 million entries with incomplete, invalid, or inconsistent data (Nicolas and Sampaio, 2024), making the foundation of automated decisions unreliable. Second, when automated denials are hard to understand and appeals are slow, formal rights stop functioning in practice: the CRPS averages 278 days to process an appeal (2025), and Meu INSS — the primary digital filing channel — creates access barriers for rural, elderly, and digitally constrained claimants who cannot navigate it independently.
What already exists
INSS + Isaac + Dataprev▾
INSS administers pensions, disability, maternity, and sickness benefits. Isaac is the AI-assisted automation layer cross-referencing applicant data against CNIS; Dataprev is the state-owned IT company that operates it. Dataprev faces a structural conflict of interest: the same entity handles both operational delivery and the technical infrastructure for scrutiny.
CRPS (Appeals Board)▾
The Social Security Appeals Board hears appeals against INSS denials. Multiple filing channels exist (Central 135, CRPS website, Meu INSS, in-person) — but average processing time is 278 days (2025), and navigation burden is high. Formal appeal rights do not translate into timely redress without binding time targets and published performance data.
CGU + TCU▾
The Comptroller General (CGU) conducts internal audits — its 2023 review identified governance and transparency weaknesses in INSS automation. The Federal Court of Audit (TCU) provides external/legislative oversight and has reviewed CNIS data quality. Both operate episodically; no standing routine audit of INSS automated decisions currently exists.
Caixa + LGPD Art. 20▾
Caixa has 4,200+ branches and service posts, 9,000+ CAIXA Aqui correspondents, and 13,000+ lottery outlets — already an informal fallback for claimants excluded from digital channels. It is not currently listed as an official CRPS intake point. LGPD Article 20 provides a legal right to request human review of purely automated decisions, but this is case-by-case, not a system-level accountability architecture.
Policy priorities
  • Place automated INSS decision-making under routine CGU audit with mandatory demographic disaggregation of denial rates — published quarterly.
  • Designate agricultural and non-standard claims (segurado especial, self-declared work periods, hazardous-condition classification, irregular contribution histories) as default human-review categories.
  • Set a binding maximum CRPS appeal processing time with published quarterly performance data on caseload, backlog, and staffing ratios.
  • Formalize Caixa branches as official INSS appeal intake and biometric verification points, with a service-level agreement and dedicated resourcing — formalising a role Caixa already plays informally.
  • Enforceable rights to human review and written explanation for automated welfare denials require PL 2338/2023 to pass — LGPD Article 20 provides only a case-by-case legal baseline, not a system-level accountability architecture.
  • Resolving the Dataprev conflict of interest — where the operational delivery entity also provides the technical infrastructure for scrutiny — requires either a legislative mandate for audit independence or an executive restructuring decision.

Diagnosis & Conclusion

Overall welfare risk profile

Most pressing risk
Adaptive — transitional displacement
Banking is one realised sectoral pressure; broader vulnerability extends across routine clerical, administrative, and office-intensive work, especially for women in high-AI-exposure occupations. The income support system still leaves a large middle group without adequate protection once existing instruments run out. Access-and-delivery risks compound this picture: INSS over-automation is already creating documented harm.
Strongest capacity dimension
Coping — delivery infrastructure
Pix, CadÚnico, and Bolsa Família together provide a delivery and targeting infrastructure that few comparable economies have. Auxílio Emergencial showed this system can move cash at population scale when political authorisation exists. The challenge is converting this crisis-response capacity into a standing, rules-based automatic stabiliser.
Most fragile capacity dimension
Adaptive — no assembled transition system
The banking sector collective agreement is exceptional. Seguro Desemprego provides income but not transition support. Sistema S mainly serves youth and incumbent workers. Workers in AI-managed employment still lack enforceable procedural rights while PL 2338/2023 remains pending. There is no rules-based connection linking income support, training finance, and placement for AI-displaced workers.
Key data gap
The missing middle is not monitored
No routine monitoring captures displaced workers above the Bolsa Família threshold. Converging proxies — 5.2 million workers at peak AI-exposure risk, 8.4 million formal workers in CadÚnico without active Bolsa Família, and a sharp employment-tenure gradient near the threshold — suggest millions are affected, but a precise count does not exist.
Four binding constraints across all risk dimensions
01
Automated INSS decisions still lack routine demographic monitoring and publishable denial disaggregation, despite CGU (2023) identifying governance and transparency weaknesses. The 278-day appeal wait means formal redress rights do not function in practice for most claimants.
02
No institutional mechanism connects RAIS, CadÚnico, and Sistema S into a proactive retraining pathway for displaced workers — even though the technical foundations exist. An inter-ministerial protocol, legal basis, and funding commitment are all required.
03
The political commitment to earmark broader tax-base gains (from VAT reform or dividend tax) toward welfare financing has not been established. Welfare financed through payroll in an increasingly capital-intensive economy faces a structural revenue problem that is not self-resolving.
04
Workers in AI-managed employment still lack enforceable procedural rights — rights to explanation, contestation, and human review in employment and social-security contexts. These depend on passage and implementation of PL 2338/2023 or an equivalent framework.
Strategic Sequencing
Near-term
Governance strengthening: routine CGU audit of INSS automation with demographic disaggregation, binding CRPS processing-time targets with published quarterly data, default human-review categories for non-standard claims, and Caixa formalised as official intake channel. Addresses a large share of current acute harms within existing institutional authority.
Medium-term
Build connective tissue for worker transition: inter-ministerial RAIS–CadÚnico data-sharing protocol, a broader Sistema S mandate for mid-career retraining, and extension of the banking CCT model to other sectors where union capacity permits. Requires new inter-ministerial coordination and legal clarification.
Longer-term
Resolve payroll dependency by directing part of the broader revenue base opened by VAT reform and dividend taxation toward welfare financing. Not achievable by administrative decision alone — requires sustained political commitment and legislative action.
